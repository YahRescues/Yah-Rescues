A claim may go something like this:

"because the final 'god' in John 1:1 doesn't have the Greek definite article, the word a may be supplied in translation"

To somebody who doesn't know about definite articles or how they occur this might be convincing.

What is a definite article?

English has a definite article, it is the word "the" It represents definiteness. If you say you are going to have the apple, there is only one apple in questionn, but if you say you will have an apple then there are multiple apples in question. So the claim is that since John 1:1 doesn't have the word "the" then the word "God" isn't definite and cannot be referring to Almighty God.

Greek Words:

θεός - God
ὁ - Article

The Greek Article



The problem is that we are dealing with Greek, and not English. We can't apply English grammatical rules and force them to apply to Greek when they don't. English has more than one article and can easier distinguish between definiteness and indefiniteness, Greek has one article and it wouldn't be fair to say every time the article doesn't appear there can be no definiteness. The Greek article is not "the Greek definite aricle" It doesn't have a definite article. It has an article which may or may not respresent definiteness. Its presence doesn't demand definiteness, its absence doesn't demand indefiniteness.

Applying the Logic

To accept the claim we would need to accept the idea that every time the word 'God' appears without the Greek article, it is not referring to Almighty God. If we applied this logic we would onlt need to get to the 4th chapter before we had a discrepency

Matthew 4:4

ἀλλ᾽ ἐπὶ παντὶ ῥήματι ἐκπορευομένῳ διὰ στόματος θεοῦ

Conclusion

The word for God looks different. Depending on how the word is being used, the ending of the word appears differently. Here we can see that the word 'God' appears without the "definite article" but it clearly refers to Almighty God. The NWT used by Jehovah's Witnesses translates this word as Jehovah. If the logic is correct then they have incorrectly put Jehovah's name in the place of another god. Do the translators really believe that the absence of the article demands indefiniteness? DO they believe that 'God' appearing without the article cannot be reffering to Almighty God? Matthew knew the passage in Hebrew, he will have known the passage used to tetragrammaton, and he used 'God' without the article to refer to Almighty God. If it is good enough for the apostles to refer to Almighty God without the Greek article, then is should be good enough for us.
